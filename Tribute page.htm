Allen Ginsberg

The life of a beatnik

Allen Ginsberg reads to the crowd in Washington Square Park in August 28, 1966

Irwin Allen Ginsberg was an American poet and writer. Best known for his poem "Howl", Ginsberg denounced what he saw as the destructive forces of capitalism and conformity in the United States. In the 1940s, he became friends with Lucien Carr, William S. Burroughs and Jack Kerouac, forming the core of the Beat Generation. Ginsberg was a Buddhist who extensively studied Eastern religious disciplines. He lived modestly, buying his clothing in second-hand stores and residing in apartments in New York City's East Village. He vigorously opposed militarism, economic materialism, and sexual repression, and he embodied various aspects of this counterculture with his views on drugs, sex, multiculturalism, hostility to bureaucracy, and openness to Eastern religions.

A time line of Allen's life

  • 1926 - born in Newark, New Yersey
  • 1943 - graduates high school and gets a scholarship to Columbia University. It was here, where he meets Jack Kerouac, William S. Burroughs and Neal Cassidy, a group of writers and intellectuals who are later refered to as the 'Beat Generation.'
  • 1948 - he claims to experience an auditory hallucination whlie reading Willian Blake, this becomes a formative experience for his later poetry and beliefs.
  • 1950's - he moves to San Francisco where works as a market researcher for a while and gets aquinted with a group of poets known as the San Franciso Rennaisance. He meests Peter Orlovsky his lifelong partner.
  • 1955 - Six Gallery reading in San Francisco. This is where Ginsberg first presents 'Howl' the poem which brought him international fame.
  • 1956 - his mother, Naomi dies. She had schizophrenia, and her mental illness had a huge effect on Giinsberg's life and poems. He wrote one of his most famous and moving poem 'Kaddish' about her passing.
  • 1957 - he abandons San Francisco, and starts travelling. This period of his life lasts for over a decade, he travels to Africa and Asia, he also lives in a shabby lodging house in Paris later known as the Beat Hotel. This is a poeticly productive period in his life.
  • 1965 - he holds readings in London, including an event in the Royal Albert Hall, which attracted over 7000 people and was a very influential event in modern English poetry. He also takes part in a student festivity in Prague, where he is elected as the 'May King' by students and was later deported from Checoslovakia by the communists.
  • 1960's - he continues to publish poetry and he forms a bridge between the beats and the hippies befriending influential figures of the era like Bob Dylan.
  • 1970's - beeing a devoted Buddhist he helps in founding the Jack Kerouac School of Disembodied Poetics at Naropa University in Boulder, Colorado. He continues to speak out against the Vietnam War and protest for gay rights and free speech issues, and continues tol do so until his death in 1997.
  • 1980's and 90's - despite experiencing serious health issues he remains active both socially and poeticly, he receives several awards and continues to help friends and artists from all around the world.
  • 1997 - He dies on April 5, 1997, surrounded by family and friends in his East Village loft in Manhattan, succumbing to liver cancer via complications of hepatitis at the age of 70 after making phone calls to say goodbye to everyone in his address book.
